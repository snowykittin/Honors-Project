    Usability Test

    Deliverables

    • Usability Test Document

    Assignment Details

    For this assignment, the only deliverable was a full usability test document. This included writing a script, having students test it with three people, and writing a conclusion based upon those usability tests. Usability testing is done to determine the way everyday people would use a program, typically in its prototype phase. As programmers, the sequence of events to be able to complete a task is extremely obvious. To customers, this is rarely ever the case. In conducting a usability test, shortcomings in the usability are brought to life. Things that are supposed to be easy to the user are tested to see if they truly are or not, and it also gives insight into how an everyday user would use the program for the tasks they are assigned. Questions that are created are not meant to lead users towards one answer or another, but instead, ask for feedback on how their experience is going. Students had to be sure to take into account several things, such as background information the users might have, experience with similar products, as well as being sure that any users involved understand their responses are anonymous, but will be used by the design team to improve the product. Students then conducted these tests based off of the wireframes they created for Assignment 2.

    Reflection

    This was another assignment where I was a little bit intimidated by the social requirement, however, learning from the first assignment, I was a lot more prepared. Usability tests are extremely important during the development cycle as it helps significantly in knowing whether or not the current trajectory of a product or a feature of a product works with the target audience. Typically conducted by UX researchers, it involves getting people in a room to actually test what is being designed and/or created. When it comes to feedback on how to improve my design, I’ve actually found this to be one of the most useful methods of gaining feedback. It’s so easy to hand something to somebody and ask them what they think. More often than not, that will lead to a rather vague answer, as people are generally empathetic and don’t like to hurt others feelings. Sadly, when it comes to improving products, that’s never really useful. Instead, putting them through the process of actually interacting with the product really gives the insight that people need. Even simply being able to observe somebody using a product gives so many useful insights that otherwise wouldn’t be gotten through a simple survey. For example, being able to see the amount of time someone takes figuring out their next task can tell if it’s too easy or too hard.

    One super interesting piece of feedback that I got during my usability tests surrounded a feature in my wireframe that I hadn’t expected to gain as much praise as it did. I had introduced a “send to phone” feature in my wireframe. To me, it just simply seemed logical. In today’s day and age, being given a set of directions often leads to them being forgotten and people getting lost. I wanted to include a way to connect to the existing technology that I had found out in my persona that people already used quite heavily – their phone. This actually led to one of my usability participants praising it from an accessibility perspective, as forgetting actually tends to be a crippling downfall. Even within a small space, having a back-up to the human memory (which is known to fail) was extremely beneficial. I also got a lot of good feedback that my design was super consistent and nice to use, which made me feel rather good. Learning more about iconography was also an unintended benefit of this usability test that I got, which helped me improve my design for the final assignment.

    If I were to do this assignment again, I would have liked to go much more in-depth with my conclusion. I felt like I really didn’t go as deep into it as I could have. I got a lot of number-based answers, which I could have gone much more in-depth with my interpretation of. Instead, I feel like I left it rather general, taking all of the positive praise, and not going nearly as in-depth with the suggestions. There is a lot of room for improvement with any design, and there were certainly some subtle suggestions I could’ve accounted for. I also think I would have asked better questions, as I really feel like I just asked the same thing over and over. I don’t really like repetition, so trying to vary things up a bit more is definitely an area I could have improved in.

    Personally, I don’t think I’ll ever be conducting another usability test. However, I certainly will still be using them in the future. They’ll be the basis on which I provide suggestions, or even program them. They have such a vital role in the design process that I am sure I will encounter them again. Of course, it is also entirely possible that I end up in a UX researcher role, and then I’ll probably be conducting more than a few of them. The value that a usability test has also is something that I plan on using in future projects. I was able to learn that I take a lot more value in being able to gain the feedback a usability test gives than simply a questionnaire. There is so much information that they give to the development team, being able to harvest all of that is certainly difficult. I really enjoyed noticing the subtle things, such as how long people took to figure out a path, how they made the connections, and how they interpreted what was in front of them. No person will ever interpret it in the exact same way as somebody else, and actually, that was really useful in being able to improve my design. There were consistencies between quite a few of the tests which completely threw off my expectations, but were extremely important in the feedback. So while I’m not a fan of having to sit people down to test a product, I know there’s so much value to a usability test, I’ll certainly be using it in some way or another in the future.
