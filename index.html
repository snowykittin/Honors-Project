    ONE STUDENTS JOURNEY IN I270

    About I270

    According to the SoIC website for IUPUI, Students learn the fundamental principles and practices of human-computer interaction (HCI) and evaluation. Specific focus is given to the introductory knowledge of HCI methods, tools, and techniques for designing and evaluating user interfaces through the use of low and high fidelity prototypes for the Web and software. As stated in the syllabus, This course will introduce Informatics undergraduates to the fields of Human-Computer Interaction (HCI). This focuses on the use of computing technology. From this perspective, we can understand how various technologies fit into users’ lives more effectively and can extend users’ cognitive capabilities through designing new experiences for them in HCI. The course will introduce you to the design process that accompanies software development.

    Furthermore, from the syllabus, the course is divided into two main activities of interaction designers. The first activity deals with the design process of software and hardware technology and the relation of our designs to the user. Examples of design include learning about our software users, sketching and brainstorming different design alternatives, and developing prototypes. The second important activity is the evaluation of the generated designs. Examples of evaluation include expert evaluation, usability studies, and field studies. There is a plethora of learning outcomes from this course. As detailed in the syllabus, one of the most foundational outcomes are the understanding and ability to explain HCI basics, interaction design, and related areas. Another is to be able to understand and explain a user-centered approach to interaction design. Overall, the goal of the course was to be able to use several different evaluation techniques for performing basic usability studies or heuristic evaluations in order to generate new, more effective design concepts.

    The design process is something that all software engineers will encounter no matter what corner of software engineering they find themselves in. As someone who has demonstrated interest both in User Experience research, as well as software programming, being able to understand both is extremely important to me for the value of communication between teams, as well as being able to better understand what it is my coworkers bring to the table. As such, this project is dedicated to delving further into the mind of a User Experience (UX) mindset, while combining it with the software programming side that I have also been honing my skills in.

    The Assignments

    Assignment #1

    The main focus surrounding this assignment was personas and scenarios. Tasked with interviewing five friends, students then had to create them into a single persona. A persona is a fictive user with goals, motivations, and challenges that may be encountered while interacting with a website. Their particular use is being able to remotely develop usage scenarios for products without actually having to go out and find real people. The given scenario, which would be continued throughout the rest of the semester, was to find a way to help create a service kiosk for members of a city to use to aid in tourist activities. Given that it was for natives to the area, it had to have features that would appeal to people who both didn't and did already live in the area.

    Assignment #2

    Building off of Assignment 1, this introduced the concept of wireframing. Prototypes can be extremely useful in being able to translate things in the brain to on paper, and helps particularly in making sure a team is on the same page about a design. They are typically extremely low-fidelity, essentially being a mock-up of the basic shapes and structures of a program. With using Whimsical, notes about basic interactions are also able to be made. In this case, wireframing the kiosk in context of going through a single task was assigned. I chose to go for the route of walking through an event, all through reserving seats and purchasing tickets. This prototype would come into use for later assignments.

    Assignment #3

    This assignment saw a slight divergence from the kiosk-based activities done for Assignment 1 and 2. It focused on creating a Heuristic Evaluation for a website of student choosing. Heuristic evaluations are a way UX researchers are able to compare a website or design to a predefined set of expectations that have generally been adopted by society. An example of this would be how everyone expects for the "X" to close something to be in the top right-hand corner if you are a Windows user, or top left-hand corner if you are a Mac user. For this particular heuristic evaluation, the Indianapolis Craigslist website was evaluated. There were quite a few interesting findings. Heuristic evaluations are a great way of showing inconsistencies in the design for how expectations may not be exactly aligning with what the designers intend. People tend to prefer not having to relearn things over and over, and so there has become a sub-conscious set of expectations found on the internet.

    Assignment #4

    Once more, students returned to the development of the tourist kiosk. Focusing on performing a usability test, students had to elect three people to test the designs they created in Assignment 2. The purpose of a usability test is exactly in the name: it checks whether or not the current design iteration is usable, as well as gives a chance for feedback to improve the design. These usability tests follow a set of questions to subconsciously guide a user through the program, but are worded in a way as to not force any opinions. In the case of this usability test, students had to walk their selected people through their prototypes with a set of questions they came up with. They then had to compile a report of their findings from these usability tests, and come up with suggestions to be able to improve the design.

    Assignment #5

    Serving as the final project, this assignment pulled from elements of all the previous ones to create a high-fidelity, final mock-up of the travel kiosk. In particular, students focused on feedback from Professor Hofmann on Assignment 2, and the suggestions that were made by their usability testers from Assignment 4. On top of enhancing their prototype, students also had to introduce an error screen for a common error that might occur while using the kiosk. The purpose of UX research is to be able to take everything that has been discovered through feedback and testing and combine it into a final design that keeps in mind the original task of the product, but also functions in a way that people find pleasing and intuitive. As such, there is the expectation that students take everything they have learned into account when creating this.
