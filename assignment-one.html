    Personas & Scenarios

    Assignment Details

    Three things were turned in for this assignment. An interview with questions and answers, a persona created off of those responses, and the scenario. One of the key things to remember when creating a persona is that it is not about the researcher. Instead, it is based off of the data from any questions asked during the interview. In the case of this assignment, students were asked to pretend they were working for the city tourism board. Focusing on how people use media and mobile devices when navigating the city, the interest was in understanding how people use media in their lives and so forth. Based upon this data, and the collected persona, a scenario based off of how local Indianapolis residents would use tourism kiosks was to be created. It was noted that the city had a basic idea how tourists would use the kiosks, but the main focus was how Indianapolis residents would use it. As such, the scenario was written from the persona's perspective, noting details about the kiosk in this scenario.

    Reflection

    Having to interview people and conduct research is probably one of my least favorite things in the world, to be quite honest. The idea of forced interactions in order to get an assignment done has always been something that I’ve struggled with, given that I fall into the category of being an introvert. However, even despite the social hurdle that I faced immediately going into this assignment, there was quite a bit to it that actually worked well within its own favor. I take quite a bit of enjoyment in being able to find the connections between various data sources. I'll go into them thinking of expecting one thing and finding several others, and this definitely happened in the case of this assignment. People are rather unpredictable, and that is probably the number one lesson I took out of this. I had also heard a little bit about the use of user personas prior to taking this course, as I was essentially our UX/UI developer for a few projects that I worked on in high school. We had to develop user cases for how our website would actually be used, doing a demo of it in the context of movies. So going into it, I had a bit of background knowledge, which made me feel a lot more comfortable with the assignment overall. Being able to choose online friends also made it significantly easier, as I am not someone with a lot of friends in-person. I’m getting better about that, but the access to people was a barrier I wasn’t anticipating having to think about at first.

    I enjoyed getting to design the persona and the kiosk scenario the most. Design is something that I don’t have the largest passion for, but I’ve got a large respect and interest in. I know that I’m not anything near a phenomenal designer, which is why I’ve opted for web design over graphics and illustrations, but I still appreciate the front-end, prettifying side of things. Getting to put together an “infographic” for my persona really honed in on that particular interest of mine. My passion for accessibility design also jumped out of nowhere when detailing the kiosk, as well. I found myself going into the accessibility details with quite a bit more enthusiasm than the rest of the kiosk’s functionality. As someone who has minor disabilities, I have a bit more reason to think about it than others. This actually ended up being something that carried through the actual design of the kiosk that was praised in a later assignment, but as that was part of a later assignment, the details for that will be found on another page.

    If I had to redo this assignment, I would try to complete it using people actually within Indianapolis. As the kiosk was centered around people who lived in Indianapolis, I had to try to tailor my questions to be as location-neutral as possible in order to get the information that I needed. I also had to make quite a few assumptions when trying to apply my persona to the kiosk, as almost all of my questionnaire participants were not from Indianapolis. I’m a writer in my free time, and so I actually felt I did quite a good job describing the scenario. However, I would like to go more in-depth, getting a better scenario in my mind before actually going through with writing it. There were a few fundamental miscommunications that found their way into my scenario document, such as the fact that I pictured these kiosks existing on the corner of a street, rather than inside of a popular location. This was something I thought of when developing my prototype and took into account, but I would have liked to have this in my mind from the beginning. I feel this would have made it a lot easier.

    Understanding the concept of personas, their position within the development cycle, and their benefits is something that I will certainly be using in my future career. Whether I end up actually committing to the UX life, or even just going into software design, it’s really useful for determining the purpose and ways a product would be used. One thing I’m rather aware of as a developer is the fact that the programmer knows the tool perfectly, and that people don’t. That’s a bias that is almost impossible to fully get rid of. Even after leaving projects alone for years, I have come back to them with full knowledge of it. Having a persona that I can reference as a personality is extremely beneficial when working on any project, as it leads to questions and benefitting any product that may be produced. Striving to be better should be a quality of any company or project, and personas really help with that process. You have to know your userbase to design for them. After all, if you were developing a product for kindergarteners without realizing how much English they know, it could quickly end badly. It’d be like marketing Twilight to a kid who only listens to Baby Shark. It doesn’t work out very well.
